Alternate exterior angles formed by parallel lines are congruent.

When two parallel lines are intersected by a transversal, alternate exterior angles are created on opposite sides of the transversal, located outside the parallel lines. These angles are congruent, meaning they have the same measure. This property arises directly from the parallel nature of the lines and the intersection created by the transversal.

To visualize this concept, consider two parallel lines, which we will label as Line A and Line B, along with a transversal line that crosses them. At each intersection of the transversal with Line A and Line B, four angles are formed. The alternate exterior angles are the pairs of angles that are positioned outside the parallel lines and on opposite sides of the transversal. For example, if the transversal intersects Line A at point $P$ and Line B at point $Q$, the angles formed at points $P$ and $Q$ that are on opposite sides of the transversal and outside the parallel lines are classified as alternate exterior angles.

This property is particularly useful in various geometric proofs and problems. For example, if you know the measure of one of the alternate exterior angles, you can easily determine the measure of the corresponding angle. This understanding aids in solving for unknown angles in more complex geometric figures.
