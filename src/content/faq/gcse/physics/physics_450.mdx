Work involves the transfer of energy by moving it from one form or object to another.

When you exert work on an object, you apply a force that causes the object to move, indicating that energy is being transferred. For instance, if you push a box across the floor, you are transferring energy from your muscles (which store chemical energy) to the box (which gains kinetic energy). The amount of energy transferred is determined by the magnitude of the force applied and the distance over which this force acts. This relationship can be expressed by the formula:

$$
\text{Work Done (Joules)} = \text{Force (Newtons)} \times \text{Distance (Metres)}.
$$

Energy can be transferred in various forms. For example, when you lift an object, you transfer energy to it as gravitational potential energy. The higher the object is lifted, the greater its gravitational potential energy becomes. Similarly, when you stretch a spring, you are transferring energy in the form of elastic potential energy.

In electrical circuits, the work done by an electric current facilitates the transfer of energy from the power source to the components within the circuit. For example, a battery converts chemical energy into electrical energy, which then powers a light bulb, transforming electrical energy into light and heat energy.
