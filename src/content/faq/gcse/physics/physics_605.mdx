Redshift is a crucial indicator of the universe's expansion, providing scientists with insights into its eventual fate.

Redshift occurs when light emitted from distant galaxies shifts towards the red end of the spectrum. This shift happens because these galaxies are moving away from us, which stretches the light waves and causes them to appear redder. The phenomenon was first documented by Edwin Hubble in the 1920s and serves as a fundamental piece of evidence for the expanding universe.

By measuring the redshift of galaxies, scientists can determine their velocity relative to us. The greater the redshift, the faster the galaxy is receding. This relationship is encapsulated in Hubble's Law, which relates the redshift to the distance and speed of galaxies. The rate of expansion offers valuable clues about the universe's past and its potential future behavior.

Understanding redshift is essential for developing models that predict the universe's fate. If the expansion continues indefinitely, it may lead to a scenario termed the "Big Freeze," in which galaxies drift so far apart that stars exhaust their fuel, resulting in a cold and dark universe. Conversely, if the expansion decelerates and eventually reverses, it could culminate in a "Big Crunch," where the universe collapses back into a hot, dense state.
