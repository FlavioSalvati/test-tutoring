A typical leaf consists of a broad, flat section known as the blade, which is attached to the plant stem via a stalk called the petiole.

The leaf's structure is specifically evolved to optimize photosynthesis, the process by which plants convert sunlight into energy. The blade, or lamina, is the primary site of photosynthesis and is typically green due to the presence of chlorophyll, a pigment that absorbs sunlight effectively.

The petiole serves to connect the blade to the stem, positioning the leaf in an optimal location to capture sunlight. It can adjust the angle of the leaf throughout the day to maximize light exposure.

The leaf is composed of several layers. The outermost layer is called the epidermis, which protects the leaf and is coated with a waxy substance known as the cuticle. This cuticle plays a crucial role in minimizing water loss. Beneath the epidermis lies the mesophyll, which is divided into two regions: the palisade mesophyll and the spongy mesophyll. The palisade mesophyll contains a high concentration of chloroplasts, where the majority of photosynthesis occurs, while the spongy mesophyll contains air spaces that facilitate gas exchange.

Veins traverse the leaf, providing structural support and transporting water, minerals, and sugars. These veins are part of the plant's vascular system, consisting of xylem and phloem. The xylem is responsible for transporting water and minerals from the roots to the other parts of the plant, while the phloem carries sugars, the byproducts of photosynthesis, from the leaves to other areas of the plant.

Stomata are tiny pores typically located on the underside of the leaf, allowing for gas exchange. They permit the entry of carbon dioxide for photosynthesis and the release of oxygen. Guard cells regulate the opening and closing of these stomata, controlling gas exchange and helping to prevent excessive water loss.
