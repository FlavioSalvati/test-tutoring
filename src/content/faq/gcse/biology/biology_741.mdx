title: "How does the contraceptive pill work?"
summary: "The contraceptive pill works by preventing ovulation and altering the lining of the uterus to prevent pregnancy."

The contraceptive pill is an oral form of hormonal contraception designed to prevent pregnancy by interfering with various reproductive processes. Commonly known as "the pill," it contains synthetic versions of the female hormones estrogen and progesterone, which are naturally produced by the ovaries. These hormones function in three primary ways to prevent pregnancy.

Firstly, the pill inhibits ovulation, which is the release of an egg from the ovaries. It does this by maintaining consistent levels of estrogen and progesterone in the bloodstream, effectively signaling to the body that it is already pregnant and, as a result, no egg is released.

Secondly, the pill thickens the cervical mucus at the neck of the womb (cervix), creating a barrier that makes it difficult for sperm to enter the uterus and reach an egg. This thickening occurs due to elevated levels of progesterone induced by the pill.

Lastly, the pill also reduces the thickness of the endometrial lining of the uterus. A thinner lining decreases the likelihood that a fertilized egg can successfully implant and develop into a pregnancy.

It is crucial to take the contraceptive pill every day to maintain stable hormone levels. Any deviation from the prescribed regimen can diminish its effectiveness. There are two primary types of contraceptive pills: the combined pill, which contains both estrogen and progesterone, and the progesterone-only pill, commonly referred to as the mini-pill. While both types work to prevent pregnancy, the mini-pill primarily functions by thickening cervical mucus and thinning the uterine lining.

In summary, the contraceptive pill employs synthetic hormones to simulate the natural menstrual cycle, effectively preventing ovulation, altering cervical mucus, and modifying the uterine lining to inhibit pregnancy.
