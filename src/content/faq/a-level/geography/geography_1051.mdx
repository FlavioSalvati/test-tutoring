The evidence supporting the theory of plate tectonics is comprehensive and encompasses geological features, fossil records, and seismic activity data.

One of the most compelling pieces of evidence comes from the geological features of the Earth. The continents appear to fit together like pieces of a jigsaw puzzle, a phenomenon first observed by Alfred Wegener in the early 20th century. This observation led to his formulation of the theory of continental drift, which serves as a precursor to the modern understanding of plate tectonics. Notably, the similarity of geological formations across different continents bolsters this theory. For instance, the Appalachian Mountains in North America closely resemble the Scottish Highlands in Europe, suggesting that these regions were once part of a contiguous landmass that has since separated.

Fossil records provide additional support for the theory of plate tectonics. Identical fossils of certain species have been discovered on widely separated continents, indicating that these landmasses were once connected. A prime example is the Mesosaurus, a freshwater reptile whose fossils have been found in both South America and Africa. Given that this creature could not have traversed the vast Atlantic Ocean, the evidence strongly suggests that the two continents were once joined.

Seismic activity data also plays a critical role in substantiating the theory. Earthquakes and volcanic eruptions are predominantly concentrated along the boundaries of tectonic plates. The distribution of seismic activity aligns closely with the boundaries of tectonic plates, providing robust evidence for their existence.

Finally, the study of the Earth's magnetic field further reinforces the theory of plate tectonics. The Earth's magnetic field has undergone several reversals throughout its history, a phenomenon known as geomagnetic reversal. Evidence of these reversals is captured in the oceanic crust. As new crust forms at mid-ocean ridges, it records the prevailing direction of the Earth's magnetic field at that time. This process has resulted in distinctive patterns of magnetic stripes on the ocean floor, which serve as a historical record of plate movement over millions of years. This magnetic evidence has been instrumental in the development and widespread acceptance of the theory of plate tectonics.
