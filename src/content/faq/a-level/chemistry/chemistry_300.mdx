First-row transition metals exhibit variable oxidation states and are known for forming colored compounds.

These metals, located in the d-block of the periodic table, include scandium, titanium, vanadium, chromium, manganese, iron, cobalt, nickel, and copper. Their unique properties stem from their partially filled d-orbitals, which enable them to absorb and reflect light, resulting in the vibrant colors of their compounds.

A significant characteristic of first-row transition metals is their variable oxidation states. This property allows them to either lose or gain electrons, leading to the formation of ions with different charges. For instance, iron can exhibit oxidation states of +2 or +3, while copper can exist in oxidation states of +1 or +2. Such versatility makes these metals invaluable in various biological and industrial applications.

These complex ions are often colored and are utilized in analytical chemistry to detect the presence of specific metals in samples.

In summary, the properties of first-row transition metals are crucial in numerous biological and industrial processes. They play essential roles in functions ranging from oxygen transport in the blood (as seen with iron) to the production of stainless steel (with chromium).
