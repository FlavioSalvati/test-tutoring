title: "What is the difference between a prion and a virus?"
summary: "Prions are infectious proteins that cause disease, whereas viruses are infectious agents composed of genetic material. Both can lead to infections but differ in structure and mechanism."

Prions and viruses are both infectious agents, but they differ significantly in their structure and mode of action.

Prions are distinctive infectious agents composed entirely of protein, lacking any genetic material. They are responsible for a variety of neurodegenerative diseases, including Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease and mad cow disease. Prions have the unique ability to induce normal proteins to misfold into their infectious form, resulting in the accumulation of these abnormal proteins within the brain and nervous system.

In contrast, viruses are infectious particles that contain genetic material, which can be either DNA or RNA, encased in a protective protein coat. They are considerably smaller than prions and have the capability to infect a diverse array of hosts, including animals, plants, and bacteria. Viruses are known to cause numerous diseases, such as the common cold, influenza, and HIV.

A notable distinction between prions and viruses is that prions are resistant to standard sterilization techniques, such as heat or radiation. This resistance makes prions particularly challenging to manage and raises concerns regarding potential contamination of medical equipment and food products.

In summary, while prions and viruses are both infectious agents, they differ fundamentally in their composition and modes of infection. Prions are infectious proteins that lead to neurodegenerative diseases, whereas viruses are infectious particles containing genetic material that can infect a wide variety of organisms.
