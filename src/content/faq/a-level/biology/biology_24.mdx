Mechanical digestion and chemical digestion are two essential processes involved in the breakdown of food.

**Mechanical Digestion** refers to the physical breakdown of food into smaller particles. This process begins in the mouth, where teeth grind and crush the food. It continues in the stomach, where muscular contractions churn the food, further reducing its size. The small intestine also contributes to mechanical digestion through rhythmic muscular contractions that mix the food with digestive juices. For a comprehensive overview of mechanical digestion, you can refer to the provided notes.

In contrast, **Chemical Digestion** involves the biochemical breakdown of food into smaller molecules that the body can absorb. This process also starts in the mouth, where saliva is secreted, containing enzymes that initiate the breakdown of carbohydrates. In the stomach, gastric juices, which include hydrochloric acid and various enzymes, work to dismantle proteins. Most of the chemical digestion occurs in the small intestine, where enzymes from the pancreas and liver further break down carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. Both mechanical and chemical digestion are vital for the body to extract nutrients from food. Mechanical digestion increases the surface area of food, facilitating the action of enzymes during chemical digestion. Chemical digestion transforms food into smaller molecules that can be readily absorbed by the body. Without these processes, the body would struggle to obtain the necessary nutrients from food. **A-Level Biology Tutor Summary:** In summary, mechanical digestion physically breaks down food into smaller pieces, beginning in the mouth and continuing through the stomach and small intestine. Chemical digestion, on the other hand, chemically transforms food into absorbable molecules, starting with saliva in the mouth and involving enzymes from the stomach and intestines. Both processes are essential for nutrient absorption, with mechanical digestion preparing food for the more intricate chemical digestion that follows.
