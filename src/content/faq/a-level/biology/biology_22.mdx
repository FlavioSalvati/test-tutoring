**Convergent and Divergent Evolution: An Overview**

Convergent evolution refers to the phenomenon where unrelated organisms develop similar traits as a response to analogous environmental pressures. This occurs when two species inhabit comparable environments and encounter similar challenges, such as predation or resource scarcity. A classic example of convergent evolution is observed in dolphins and sharks; despite being entirely unrelated, both have evolved streamlined bodies and fins that enhance their swimming efficiency. Another example can be seen in birds and bats, which have independently developed wings for flight, despite their distinct evolutionary lineages.

For further details on the interactions between different species, please refer to our notes on species interactions.

On the other hand, divergent evolution occurs when related organisms develop distinct traits due to varying environmental pressures. This process often begins when a species is divided into two or more populations that become isolated from one another. Each of these populations then faces different environmental challenges, leading to the development of unique traits that better suit their specific surroundings. This process is intricately linked to the concept of adaptive radiation.

**A-Level Biology Tutor Summary:** 
Convergent evolution occurs when unrelated species develop similar traits due to similar environmental pressures, as exemplified by the streamlined bodies of dolphins and sharks. Divergent evolution, in contrast, happens when related species evolve different traits in response to distinct environmental conditions, such as the varying beak shapes of Darwin's finches based on the food sources available on different islands.
