Gene editing is the process of modifying DNA sequences to change the expression of specific genes.

This powerful technology serves as a crucial tool for genome modification and gene therapy, enabling scientists to precisely target and alter particular genes for a variety of applications. For instance, gene editing can be employed to correct genetic mutations responsible for diseases such as sickle cell anemia and cystic fibrosis. By altering the DNA sequence, researchers can rectify these mutations and restore normal gene function.

Another significant application of gene editing lies in the creation of genetically modified organisms (GMOs). Through gene editing, it is possible to introduce new traits into plants or animals, such as enhanced disease resistance or increased agricultural yield. This capability has the potential to transform agriculture and enhance food security globally.

There are several techniques available for gene editing, with one of the most prominent being CRISPR-Cas9. This innovative system utilizes a guide RNA to locate a specific DNA sequence and employs an enzyme known as Cas9 to cleave the DNA at that precise location. Following this cut, the cell's inherent repair mechanisms can be harnessed to incorporate the desired changes into the DNA sequence.
