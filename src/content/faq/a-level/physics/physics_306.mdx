title: "How do we know the universe is expanding?"

The universe is expanding, and this phenomenon is evidenced by the redshift of light emitted from distant galaxies.

In the early 20th century, astronomer Edwin Hubble made a groundbreaking observation: the light from distant galaxies exhibited a shift towards the red end of the spectrum. This redshift is a result of the Doppler effect, which occurs when an object is moving away from an observer. Hubble interpreted this redshift as a sign that the universe is expanding, as it indicated that galaxies are receding from us at increasing velocities.

Additional evidence supporting the expansion of the universe comes from the cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB). This radiation is a remnant from the Big Bang and is remarkably uniform in all directions. However, slight temperature variations exist, with some regions being cooler than others. These fluctuations suggest that the universe has undergone expansion since the radiation was first emitted.

Moreover, the expansion of the universe is further corroborated by observations of supernovae, particularly Type Ia supernovae. These supernovae are especially valuable to astronomers because they possess a consistent luminosity. By measuring their apparent brightness, scientists can determine their distance from Earth. When this distance is compared to the redshift of the emitted light, it becomes evident that the universe is expanding at an accelerating rate.

In summary, the evidence for the expanding universe is compelling. The redshift of light from distant galaxies, the cosmic microwave background radiation, and observations of supernovae all converge on the same conclusion. This monumental discovery has transformed our understanding of the universe and paved the way for new theories, including the concept of dark energy.
