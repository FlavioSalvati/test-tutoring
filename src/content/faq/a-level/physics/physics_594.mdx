Laminar and turbulent fluid flows represent two distinct behaviors of fluid motion, each with unique characteristics.

**Laminar Flow**  
Laminar flow is defined by the smooth and orderly movement of fluid particles that travel in parallel layers. In this regime, there is minimal to no mixing between the layers, resulting in a well-organized flow pattern. Laminar flow is typically observed in low-speed and low-viscosity fluids, such as water flowing through a pipe. In this scenario, the fluid moves in straight lines without any swirling or eddying.

**Turbulent Flow**  
Conversely, turbulent flow is characterized by chaotic and unpredictable movement of fluid particles. This type of flow is prevalent in high-speed and high-viscosity fluids, such as air moving over an airplane wing. In turbulent flow, fluid particles exhibit random and irregular motion, resulting in swirling, eddying, and significant mixing between layers.

**Transition Between Flow Types**  
The transition from laminar to turbulent flow is influenced by several factors, including fluid velocity, viscosity, and the size and shape of the object through which the fluid is flowing. At lower velocities and viscosities, laminar flow is the dominant behavior, whereas at higher velocities and viscosities, turbulent flow becomes more prevalent.
