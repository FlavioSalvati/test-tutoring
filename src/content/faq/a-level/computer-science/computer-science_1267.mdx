---
title: "What is the difference between a mesh and a star topology?"
summary: "A mesh topology connects all devices to each other for redundancy, while a star topology connects all devices to a central hub."

---

A mesh topology connects all devices directly to each other, providing redundancy, while a star topology connects all devices to a central hub.

In a mesh topology, every device in the network is interconnected with every other device. This configuration creates multiple paths for data transmission between any two devices, enhancing the reliability of the network. If one connection fails, data can still be transmitted via an alternate route. This redundancy contributes to the robustness of mesh networks, making them ideal for environments where network reliability is critical, such as data centers or telecommunications networks. However, the complexity and cost associated with setting up and maintaining a mesh topology can be significant.

Conversely, in a star topology, all devices are connected to a central hub or switch. This hub serves as the intermediary for message transmission. When a device wants to send data to another device, it forwards the data to the hub, which then relays it to the intended recipient. Star networks are relatively straightforward and inexpensive to set up and maintain. However, a notable drawback is that if the central hub fails, the entire network becomes inoperable. Star topologies are commonly utilized in home networks and small businesses due to their simplicity.

Regarding performance, mesh networks can provide higher speeds and lower latency compared to star networks, as data can be sent directly between devices without the need to pass through a central hub. Nevertheless, as more devices are added, the performance of a mesh network may degrade due to increased complexity and the potential for data collisions.

In contrast, the performance of a star network primarily depends on the capacity of the central hub. A sufficiently powerful hub can manage a large volume of traffic without causing delays. However, if the hub becomes overloaded, it may create a bottleneck, resulting in a slowdown of the entire network.

In conclusion, the choice between a mesh and a star topology should be based on the specific requirements and resources of the network. Mesh networks offer enhanced reliability and potentially superior performance, albeit at the cost of greater complexity and expense. Star networks are easier and cheaper to establish, but they are less reliable and may experience performance issues if the central hub lacks adequate power.
