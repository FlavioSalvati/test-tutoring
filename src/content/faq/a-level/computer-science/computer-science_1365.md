A Network Interface Card (NIC) is a vital hardware component that enables a computer to connect to a network.

Commonly referred to as a network card, network adapter, or LAN adapter, the NIC serves as the essential interface between the computer and the network. Typically installed in one of the computer's expansion slots, the NIC is designed to facilitate the sending and receiving of data across the network.

The NIC performs several crucial functions. First and foremost, it converts the data generated by the computer into a format suitable for transmission over the network, a process known as data encapsulation. Additionally, the NIC assigns a unique hardware address, known as the Media Access Control (MAC) address, to each data packet. This address is essential for identifying both the source and destination of each packet within the network.

Moreover, the NIC manages the data transmission process. It regulates the flow of data to and from the computer, ensuring that data packets are sent and received in the correct order and without errors. In these instances, the NIC employs a technique called collision detection to identify when a collision occurs and to initiate the retransmission of the affected data.

Additionally, the NIC contributes to network security. Some NICs come equipped with built-in features that can filter out unwanted traffic or detect and respond to network threats. For instance, a NIC may include a firewall to prevent unauthorized access to the computer or support encryption to safeguard the data being transmitted.
