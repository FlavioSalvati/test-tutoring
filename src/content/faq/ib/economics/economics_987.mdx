---
title: "How does market power contribute to market failure?"
summary: "Market power contributes to market failure by enabling firms to manipulate prices and limit competition, leading to inefficiency."

---

Market power plays a significant role in market failure by enabling firms to manipulate prices and restrict competition, ultimately resulting in inefficiencies.

Market power is defined as the capacity of a firm to influence the price of a product or service within the market. This power is typically concentrated in monopolistic or oligopolistic markets, where there are few competitors. When a firm possesses substantial market power, it can establish prices that exceed the competitive equilibrium, which leads to allocative inefficiency. This scenario is a clear example of market failure, as resources are not being distributed in the most effective manner.

In a perfectly competitive market, prices are determined by the forces of supply and demand. However, when a firm wields market power, it can set prices higher than the marginal cost of production, resulting in a deadweight loss. This deadweight loss represents the loss of economic efficiency that occurs when the market does not reach its equilibrium outcome. In such cases, the profit-maximizing price set by the firm is above the socially optimal level, which leads to a reduction in the consumption of the good or service.

Additionally, firms with market power may engage in anti-competitive practices, such as predatory pricing or exclusive contracts, to suppress competition. This behavior can result in a decrease in innovation and consumer choice in the market, further exacerbating market failure. For example, a monopolist might temporarily lower prices to eliminate competitors from the market and subsequently raise prices once it has secured a dominant position. Such practices hinder new firms from entering the market, stifling both competition and innovation.

Market power can also contribute to income inequality, representing another dimension of market failure. When firms have the ability to set prices above competitive levels, they can generate supernormal profits, leading to a concentration of wealth. This situation often exacerbates income inequality, as the owners of the firm accumulate higher profits at the expense of consumers, who face elevated prices.

In summary, market power contributes to market failure through multiple channels. It allows firms to establish prices above competitive levels, resulting in allocative inefficiency and deadweight loss. Furthermore, it can restrict competition and innovation while contributing to income inequality.
