---
title: "Why does increasing temperature generally increase the reaction rate?"
summary: "Increasing temperature generally increases the reaction rate because it provides more energy, causing particles to move faster and collide more frequently and forcefully."
author: "Prof. Sophie Harris"
degree: "PhD in Organic Chemistry, University of Cambridge"

---

Increasing temperature typically enhances the reaction rate of chemical processes. This occurs because higher temperatures provide additional energy, which accelerates the movement of particles, resulting in more frequent and forceful collisions.

To elaborate, the rate of a chemical reaction is influenced by both the frequency and the energy of collisions between the reacting particles. As the temperature rises, the kinetic energy of these particles increases. Consequently, the particles move more rapidly and collide with greater frequency. An increase in collisions raises the likelihood of successful reactions, thereby elevating the overall reaction rate.

Furthermore, the increase in kinetic energy leads to more forceful collisions. In any chemical reaction, there exists a certain minimum energy threshold known as the activation energy, which must be surpassed for the reaction to proceed. This activation energy serves as an energy barrier that the reactants must overcome in order to transform into products. With higher temperatures, a greater number of particles possess sufficient energy to exceed this barrier, resulting in a higher proportion of successful collisions and an increased reaction rate.

It's also essential to consider the role of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution in this context. This statistical distribution illustrates the range of energies that particles within a system can possess at a given temperature. At elevated temperatures, the distribution shifts towards higher energy values, signifying that more particles have enough energy to surpass the activation energy threshold. This shift further explains why an increase in temperature correlates with an increased reaction rate.

In conclusion, raising the temperature enhances the reaction rate by supplying more energy to the particles, leading to faster movement and more frequent and forceful collisions, which in turn results in a greater number of successful reactions. Additionally, the shift in the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution towards higher energies at elevated temperatures plays a significant role in this phenomenon.
