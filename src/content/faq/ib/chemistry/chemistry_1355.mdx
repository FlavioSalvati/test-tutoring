A double bond plays a crucial role in the absorption of light in the ultraviolet-visible (UV-Vis) spectrum, resulting in a shift of the absorption peak to longer wavelengths.

In UV-Vis spectroscopy, the presence of a double bond within a molecule can significantly influence the spectral results. This influence arises because double bonds have the ability to absorb light in both the ultraviolet and visible regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. The absorption of light by a molecule is fundamentally tied to its electronic structure; the introduction of double bonds modifies this structure, leading to observable changes in the absorption spectrum.

The process of light absorption in UV-Vis spectroscopy involves the excitation of electrons from a lower energy state to a higher one. In molecules that contain double bonds, the $\pi$ electrons—those involved in the double bond—can be excited to the $\pi^*$ (pi-star) antibonding level. This transition requires less energy compared to the excitation of $\sigma$ electrons found in single bonds, allowing it to occur at longer wavelengths. Consequently, the presence of a double bond results in a shift of the absorption peak to longer wavelengths, a phenomenon referred to as redshift.

Furthermore, the number of double bonds within a molecule can also influence the UV-Vis spectrum. In conjugated systems, where alternating single and double bonds enable the delocalization of $\pi$ electrons across multiple adjacent atoms, absorption occurs at even longer wavelengths. In these systems, the energy gap between the ground state and the excited state is reduced, necessitating less energy (and thus longer wavelengths) for the electronic transition. To delve deeper into the behavior of electrons in such systems, consider exploring the principles of conjugation.

In summary, double bonds significantly affect the results of UV-Vis spectroscopy by enhancing light absorption and shifting the absorption peak to longer wavelengths. This effect is particularly pronounced in conjugated systems with multiple alternating single and double bonds.
