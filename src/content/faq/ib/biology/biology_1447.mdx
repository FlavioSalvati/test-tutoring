---
title: "How does antidiuretic hormone regulate the reabsorption of water in the kidney?"
summary: "Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) regulates water reabsorption in the kidneys by increasing the permeability of the collecting ducts."
author: "Prof. Thomas Green"

---

Antidiuretic hormone (ADH), also known as vasopressin, is a critical regulator of water reabsorption in the kidneys. It accomplishes this by enhancing the permeability of the collecting ducts.

ADH is synthesized in the hypothalamus and stored in the posterior pituitary gland. This hormone is essential for maintaining the body's fluid balance. When the body experiences dehydration or when blood osmolarity increases, the hypothalamus prompts the release of ADH into the bloodstream.

Once in circulation, ADH targets the collecting ducts of the kidneys, which represent the final segment of the nephron where urine is formed before excretion. Upon reaching the collecting duct cells, ADH binds to specific receptors, initiating a series of intracellular events. These events lead to the insertion of specialized water channels, known as aquaporins, into the cell membrane. The presence of these aquaporins significantly enhances the membrane's permeability to water.

Consequently, more water is reabsorbed from the urine back into the bloodstream, which dilutes the blood and decreases its osmolarity. The urine, now more concentrated and rich in waste products, is subsequently excreted from the body. This mechanism is vital for conserving water, particularly during states of dehydration or elevated blood osmolarity.

Conversely, when the body is adequately hydrated and blood osmolarity is low, the release of ADH diminishes. This reduction leads to fewer aquaporins being present in the collecting duct cells, resulting in decreased water reabsorption. As a result, the urine becomes more dilute, and excess water is expelled from the body.

In summary, ADH plays an indispensable role in regulating the body's water homeostasis. By modulating the permeability of the collecting ducts in the kidneys, it finely tunes the amount of water reabsorbed from urine back into the bloodstream, thereby ensuring that blood concentration remains within a healthy range.
