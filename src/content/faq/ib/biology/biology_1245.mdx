title: "How are sister chromatids different from homologous chromosomes?"

Sister chromatids are identical copies of a single chromosome, whereas homologous chromosomes are pairs of chromosomes that are similar but not identical.

Sister chromatids are formed during the S phase of the cell cycle, when a chromosome undergoes replication. This process results in two identical copies of the chromosome, which remain connected at a region known as the centromere. These copies are referred to as sister chromatids, and they contain the exact same genetic information because they originate from a single chromosome. During cell division, the sister chromatids are separated, ensuring that each daughter cell receives one chromatid.

In contrast, homologous chromosomes consist of a pair of chromosomes—one inherited from each parent. While they are similar in size, shape, and gene content, they are not identical. Homologous chromosomes carry the same genes in the same order, but the specific versions of these genes, known as alleles, may differ between the two chromosomes. For instance, one chromosome might carry an allele for blue eyes, while its homologous counterpart carries an allele for brown eyes. During meiosis, homologous chromosomes align and can exchange segments of DNA in a process called crossing over, which contributes to genetic variation in the offspring.

In summary, both sister chromatids and homologous chromosomes play crucial roles in cell division and genetic inheritance, but they differ in their origin and genetic composition. Sister chromatids are identical copies of a single chromosome, produced through replication during the cell cycle. Homologous chromosomes, on the other hand, are similar but not identical pairs of chromosomes—one from each parent—carrying the same genes but potentially harboring different alleles.
